Chapter 1


    It was exciting and overwhelming. The wave of emotions and the rush of butterflies that make you sick to your stomach, yet it’s like the best feeling to ever exist. It was what I had felt when I met her. 

I lost grip of my binder full of loose sheets of paper right in front of the school’s entrance; all of them went flying when the binder hit the ground. I dropped to my knees and started sliding documents closer when my hand brushed against someone else’s as we reached for the same sheet. Their hand recoiled almost instantly. 

“Sorry,” they apologized. “Thought you might’ve needed help.” Slowly, edging the paper towards me, I lifted my head to look at them. At that moment, time felt like it had stopped. She was, and this is probably an understatement, breathtaking. Her middle-parted hair fell to her shoulders, and her eyes had this amazingly captivating, thoughtful appearance. She wore a slim-fitting shirt, and a thin, gold-chain necklace dangled from her neck; her dark brunette hair was tucked neatly behind her ears. Her grace and elegance seemed natural– she didn’t make an effort to look good; she simply did. In contrast, I was a complete mess, with my short, scruffy hair and my ill-fitting all-around outfit.

“Thanks for, uh, trying to help. But, I’m- I’m all right,” I barked a short laugh–my face burning. In response, she gave a small chuckle that, simply put, melted me. I could feel my heart pounding as I stood up to dust myself off.

I forgot that we weren’t alone; kids laughed in the distance, and conversation drifted with the breeze. But I didn’t care. I wanted to gaze at her forever as the wind blew her hair gently in her face; her tan skin glimmered in the minuscule flecks of sunlight let through by the towering magnolias.

Only the universe knows how she managed to have so much class by merely holding her hand out. “I’m Ophelia.”

I hesitated. I took her hand and shook it lightly. “Allysa,” I said. In a brief moment of silence, I recalled what we were doing in the first place and returned to gathering papers.

“You’re blushing,” she spoke in a rather bold tone, the corners of her mouth lifting subtly. Immediately, I dropped my head and aggressively brushed my bangs further downward.


And then the Biblical Apocalypse began.


The sky turned a dark shade of red, and sheets of an acidic red liquid poured down. A demonic wail from off in the distance echoed through the campus, and thundering footsteps ensued. Swarms of tiny, insectoid creatures flew overhead, occasionally descending to pick off all the flesh of any living being, leaving clusters of lifeless skeletons in their wake. The ground started to convulse violently as massive worm-like creatures carved into the terrain underfoot, creating extensive networks of tunnel systems. The wind abruptly turned from a light breeze to a severe gale, blowing away all my recollected papers. Behind us, a ragged figure rose from beneath the ground. 

Quickly, I got to my feet with my backpack clutched tightly in my hand. “Behind you. Ophelia.” Suddenly, its pace quickened, and soon, it was racing toward us at a terrifyingly high speed. I struck the abomination with as much force as I could muster in a short backswing. The thing fell. Thick, viscous blood splattered across the floor as its head collided with the concrete.

Caustic droplets of rain spattered onto my arm, taking immense willpower to resist screaming in agony. Ignoring the pain, I slipped off my argyle sweater and gave it to Ophelia as weak protection, but protection nonetheless. I took her by the wrist, and despite apparently being in great danger, I reflexively smirked. 

“Come on,” I urged her. “We have to find cover.” As we ran, we set our destination to the gated entrance of the campus. I gripped the broad handle with both hands and pulled as hard as I could, but to no avail.

“Oh my god, you’re hurt,” Ophelia cried, pointing to the greenish-red burn mark on my left forearm, my sweater still shielding her from the rain, although it was starting to burn through. 

I kept on pulling, but the door refused to move. “I’m fine,” I grit my teeth. “But, the door’s locked. We’ll have to climb under.” I noticed a faint, sustaining buzzing sound, which seemed to gradually get louder in volume as if its source were closing in on us. I spun around and was greeted by a pleasant sight: a horde of small insectoids rushing toward us.

“Maybe, we should hurry up,” Ophelia laughed nervously. 

I dropped to the ground, my chest flat on the pavement. Just barely, I managed to slide underneath, though not without the sharp points of the fencing grazing my back. A metal awning covered the gate, so, fortunately, no other burns were attained. “Your turn,” I said. She quickly followed suit, but the disgusting creatures were getting dangerously close. Around three-quarters of the way there, she let out a mortifying scream. With their claw-like mouths, the insectoids bit her by the ankles and began dragging her back, her arms flailing in distress. In an extreme panic, I surged forward, seized her forearms, dug my heels into the ground, and started to pull.

It was like a game of tug-of-war. One side gaining over the other then vice versa, constantly back and forth. After what felt like an eternity, the creatures appeared, to retreat, letting go of their grip on their prey. Without an opposing force to balance me, I stumbled backward and she fell on top of me. Our faces were mere inches apart. We locked eyes for a moment, then I awkwardly scrambled out from underneath her. 

I pulled her up to her feet, but she couldn’t hold herself. “It hurts, really badly,” she winces. Deep, dark gashes lined her calves.

“Don’t panic,” I told her. “It’s not that bad. Just- don’t look. If you can’t stand, I’ll have to carry you.” 


Chapter 2


 I lifted her with immense struggle, holding her in my arms. She was so close that I could feel her breath on my neck. Somehow, she still held the almost completely disintegrated sweater above her. 

“How is it,” she muttered softly, “that while we’re probably in an apocalypse, your face is beet-red?”

“Is it?” I said sheepishly. “Keep holding out for just a little longer.”

As we entered the school, it was eerily silent. What was once bustling and well-populated with students and professors in the early morning was now silent or, ironically described, dead. The walls were partially coated with blood–splatter prints all around the puddles. Piles of bodies lay motionless in the halls, some still barely breathing, others completely lifeless. The buzzing noise returned, but from a comfortably far distance.

“We have to find someplace to hide from those things.” 


Behind us, loud footsteps, accompanied by low moaning, reverberated through the halls. I quickened my pace as the footsteps grew louder and more frequent. “Quick,” she whispered in my ear, her tone panicked. She pointed to a white postern in between two glass classroom doors. 

I grasped the doorknob and carefully turned it counterclockwise. As the door opened, a loud creak emanated from the hinges. The resounding footsteps stopped instantly. Whatever their source was had heard our entry; with this in mind, I darted into the closet and slowly, though urgently, closed it with a soft click. 

To call the room cramped was more than an understatement. The dimensions, I could estimate, could be around six by four feet. And if that wasn’t compact enough, bulky equipment was scattered around the floor, and a metal shelf took up a good two feet of the back end. I leaned her against a ladder and flipped the lights on. 

“Look away.”

Blood had caked her calf, but luckily, the flow of blood had seemed to slow down. Scanning the shelf, I found a satisfactorily thick towel and laid it over most of the cuts. Then I unbuttoned my dress shirt, leaving only my gray tank top underneath, and wrapped it tightly around her leg. To break the ice, her eyes still closed, she said, “Considering our circumstances, a broom closet isn’t so bad.”

I’m sure it won’t seem quite so good after a few hours here.

I tucked the last bit of shirt into the space between the rest wrapped around her leg. “You can open your eyes now.”

Her eyes fluttered open. So gracefully, so gently. The thought ran through my head a couple of times before I remembered it wasn’t the time to fawn over her. Our lives were likely at stake.
