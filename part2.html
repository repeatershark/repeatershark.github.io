Chapter 1 (continued)


The sky turned a dark shade of red, and sheets of an acidic red liquid poured down. A demonic wail from off in the distance echoed through the campus, and thundering footsteps ensued. Swarms of tiny, insectoid creatures flew overhead, occasionally descending to pick off all the flesh of any living being, leaving clusters of lifeless skeletons in their wake. The ground started to convulse violently as massive worm-like creatures carved into the terrain underfoot, creating extensive networks of tunnel systems. The wind abruptly turned from a light breeze to a severe gale, blowing away all my recollected papers. Behind us, a ragged figure rose from beneath the ground. 

Quickly, I got to my feet with my backpack clutched tightly in my hand. “Behind you. Ophelia.” Suddenly, its pace quickened, and soon, it was racing toward us at a terrifyingly high speed. I struck the abomination with as much force as I could muster in a short backswing. The thing fell, thick, viscous blood splattered across the floor as its head collided with the concrete.

Caustic droplets of rain spattered onto my arm, it took an immense amount of willpower to resist screaming in agony. Ignoring the pain, I slipped off my argyle sweater and gave it to Ophelia as weak protection, but protection nonetheless. I took her by the wrist– and despite apparently being in great danger–I reflexively smirked. 

“Come on,” I urged her. “We have to find cover.” As we ran, we set our destination to the gated entrance of the campus. I gripped the broad handle with both hands and pulled as hard as I could, but to no avail.

“Oh my god, you’re hurt,” Ophelia cried, pointing to the greenish-red burn mark on my left forearm, my sweater still shielding her from the rain, although it was starting to burn through. 

I kept on pulling, but the door refused to move. “I’m fine,” I grit my teeth. “But, the door’s locked. We’ll have to climb under.” I noticed a faint, sustaining buzzing sound, which seemed to gradually get louder in volume as if its source were closing in on us. I spun around and was greeted by a pleasant sight: a horde of the aforementioned small insectoids rushing toward us.

“Maybe, we should hurry up,” Ophelia laughs nervously. 

I drop to the ground, my chest flat on the pavement. Just barely, I manage to slide underneath, though not without the sharp points of the fencing grazing my back. A metal awning covered the gate, so, fortunately, no other burns were attained. “Your turn,” I said. She did the same, but the flying creatures of disgust were getting dangerously close. Around three-quarters of the way there, she let out a mortifying scream. The insectoids, with their claw-like mouths, bit her by the ankles and began dragging her back, her arms flailing in distress. In an extreme panic, I surged forward, seized her forearms, dug my heels into the ground, and started to pull.

It was like a game of tug-of-war. One side gaining over the other then vice versa, constantly back and forth. After what felt like an eternity, the creatures appeared to retreat, letting go of their grip on Ophelia. Without an opposing force to balance me, I stumbled backward and Ophelia fell on top of me. Our faces were mere inches apart. We locked eyes for a moment, then I awkwardly scrambled out from underneath her. 

I pulled her up to her feet, but she couldn’t hold herself. “It hurts, really badly,” she winces. Deep, dark gashes lined her calves.

“Don’t panic,” I told her. “It’s not that bad. If you can’t stand, I’ll have to carry you.”
